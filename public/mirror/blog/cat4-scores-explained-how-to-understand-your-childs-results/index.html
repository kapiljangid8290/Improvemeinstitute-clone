CAT4 Scores Explained: How to Understand Your Child's Results

2026-04-174 min read

If your child has recently taken the CAT4 exam, you may have received a report filled with terms like SAS, Stanine, and Percentile Rank. For many parents, these results can feel confusing at first. This guide breaks down what CAT4 scores actually mean, how to interpret them, and what they say about your child's learning potential.

What Do CAT4 Scores Measure?

The CAT4 (Cognitive Abilities Test) measures how a student thinks and processes information across four key areas:

  • Verbal Reasoning
  • Non-Verbal Reasoning
  • Quantitative Reasoning
  • Spatial Ability

Each of these areas is scored separately, giving a detailed picture of your child's cognitive strengths and preferences.

Understanding the Key Score Types

When you receive a CAT4 report, you will typically see three main types of scores:

1. Standard Age Score (SAS)

The SAS is the most important CAT4 score to focus on. The average score is 100, most students score between 85 and 115, scores above 115 indicate above-average ability, and scores below 85 may suggest an area where support could help. Because SAS is age-standardised, it compares your child with students of the same age group.

2. Stanine Score

Stanine scores range from 1 to 9 and group students into broad performance bands. Scores of 1 to 3 are below average, 4 to 6 sit in the average range, and 7 to 9 are above average. This gives schools a quick way to interpret where a student stands.

3. National Percentile Rank (NPR)

The NPR shows how your child compares with other students. The 50th percentile is average, the 75th percentile means your child performed better than 75% of students, and the 90th percentile indicates a top-performing range.

What Do These Scores Actually Mean?

CAT4 is not a pass/fail test. It helps schools understand how your child learns best, where their strengths lie, and which areas may need more development.

  • Strong verbal but weaker quantitative scores may suggest a preference for language-based learning.
  • High spatial ability may point towards strengths in design, engineering, or problem-solving.

How Schools Use CAT4 Results

Schools in Dubai use CAT4 data in several ways:

  • Placing students in appropriate ability groups
  • Identifying gifted or high-potential students
  • Providing additional learning support where needed
  • Tailoring teaching methods to suit different learning styles

In many cases, CAT4 results help teachers understand why a student may be overperforming or underperforming in class.

Should You Be Concerned About Low Scores?

A lower score in one area does not mean a child lacks ability. It usually highlights a skill that may need more practice or a different learning preference. With the right support and targeted preparation, students can become more confident and improve how they handle CAT4-style questions.

How Preparation Impacts Results

Although CAT4 does not test school knowledge, preparation can still make a significant difference. Students who practise become familiar with question formats, improve speed and accuracy, and build confidence in unfamiliar situations.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good CAT4 score?

A score around 100 is average. Scores above 115 are considered above average, and 120+ can indicate high ability.

Can CAT4 scores improve over time?

Yes. With practice and familiarity, students can improve how they approach reasoning questions and timed CAT4 tasks.

Do schools rely only on CAT4 scores?

No. Schools use CAT4 alongside school performance, teacher assessments, and other academic data.
